The New Corporate Transparency Act:
What Business Owners Must Know in 2026

Richard Langford 8 min read

The Corporate Transparency Act (CTA), enacted as part of the National Defense Authorization Act, has fundamentally changed the compliance landscape for millions of U.S. businesses. With significant penalties for non-compliance, understanding your obligations is no longer optional.

What Is the Corporate Transparency Act?

The CTA requires certain companies to report information about their beneficial owners — the individuals who ultimately own or control the company — to the Financial Crimes Enforcement Network (FinCEN), a bureau of the U.S. Department of the Treasury.

The law was designed to combat money laundering, tax fraud, and other illicit financial activities by increasing transparency around corporate ownership structures.

Who Must Comply?

The reporting requirements apply broadly to "reporting companies," which include:

  • Domestic corporations, LLCs, and similar entities created by filing with a state
  • Foreign entities registered to do business in the United States

However, there are 23 categories of exemptions, including publicly traded companies, banks, credit unions, and large operating companies that meet specific criteria. It is critical to assess whether your business qualifies for an exemption before assuming compliance is not required.

What Information Must Be Reported?

For each beneficial owner, companies must report:

  • Full legal name
  • Date of birth
  • Current residential or business street address
  • A unique identifying number from an acceptable document (e.g., passport, driver's license)
  • An image of the identifying document

Key Deadlines

Deadlines vary based on when your company was formed or registered:

  • Companies formed before January 1, 2024 — had until January 1, 2025 to file initial reports
  • Companies formed in 2024 — had 90 days from formation to file
  • Companies formed on or after January 1, 2025 — must file within 30 days of formation

Penalties for Non-Compliance

The penalties are severe. Willful failure to report or providing false information can result in civil penalties of up to $591 per day (adjusted for inflation) and criminal penalties of up to $10,000 in fines and two years imprisonment.

What You Should Do Now

If you have not yet assessed your CTA obligations, act immediately. Our corporate team recommends the following steps:

  1. Determine whether your company is a "reporting company" under the CTA
  2. Identify all beneficial owners (generally those owning 25% or more, or with substantial control)
  3. Gather the required identifying information and documentation
  4. File your Beneficial Ownership Information (BOI) report with FinCEN
  5. Establish a process for updating reports when ownership information changes

Given the complexity of beneficial ownership analysis and the severity of penalties, we strongly recommend consulting with a corporate attorney before filing.
